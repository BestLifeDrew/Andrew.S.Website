Overview

The world's largest student rocket competition

The American Rocketry Challenge (ARC) is a national competition organized by the Aerospace Industries Association and the National Association of Rocketry. Over 5,000 students from across the United States compete annually to design, build, and launch model rockets that must reach a precise target altitude — within a narrow scoring window — and return safely with an egg payload intact.

As a member of the IECHS rocketry team, I was involved in the full engineering design cycle: from initial concept sketches and fin geometry selection, through computer simulation and motor selection, to physical construction and live launch. Every decision — from airframe diameter to fin sweep angle — had measurable consequences at the launch pad.

Competition Rules

ARC design constraints

ARC imposes strict engineering constraints that the rocket must satisfy — not just performance targets. These rules drive every design decision and require iterative simulation to balance competing requirements.

// ARC 2024–25 Key Requirements

Mass ............. must not exceed 650 g at liftoff

Length ........... ≥ 650 mm (25.6 in) — must be above 650 mm

Body diameter .... ≥ 47 mm outside diameter

Staging .......... single stage only

Recovery ......... must come down in one piece

Motor retention .. positive retention method required (screw-on)

Beyond structural rules, the scoring is based on achieving a target altitude within the tightest possible margin — meaning simulation accuracy directly correlates to competition score.

Design Notebook

Fin geometry exploration

Early in the design phase, the team systematically explored multiple fin geometries to understand the aerodynamic tradeoffs. Fin shape affects both drag (which limits altitude) and stability margin (which determines whether the rocket flies straight). The design notebook captures this process.

Fin shapes evaluated include rectangular, trapezoidal (standard and modified), swept, and clipped delta configurations. Engineering Process

Simulation → CAD → Build → Launch

01

Flight Simulation (RocketSim)

Used RocketSim software to model rocket geometry, input motor thrust curves, and simulate flight trajectories. Iterated fin span, root/tip chord lengths, and body tube length to dial in the target apogee. Simulated across multiple motor candidates to find the best altitude-to-mass tradeoff.

02

CAD Modeling

Translated the finalized geometry into 3D CAD models to verify part dimensions before cutting. CAD allowed precise checking of body tube length, nosecone profile, and fin attachment geometry, catching fitment issues before any physical material was cut.

03

Motor Selection & Mass Budget

Evaluated multiple Estes and similar commercial motor designations (e.g., C6-5, D12-5) based on total impulse, thrust duration, and delay timing. Balanced motor impulse against the 650g mass constraint, including propellant weight. Delay timing was selected to deploy the recovery system near apogee.

04

Physical Construction

Built the airframe from cardboard body tubes, balsa fin stock, and a plastic nosecone. Fins were sanded to a symmetric airfoil profile and epoxied with reinforcing fillets. Motor mount was secured with a positive screw-on retention clip per ARC regulations. Shock cord and parachute recovery system assembled to return the rocket in one piece.

05

Launch Day

Conducted live launches at a Fayetteville field site. Verified stability visually on the pad, confirmed motor retention, and executed launch using an electric igniter. Collected flight data and compared actual altitude (via altimeter) against RocketSim predictions to validate simulation accuracy.
